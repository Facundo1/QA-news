Vaccine Misinformation Targets Hispanic and Black Communities

False information, presented on social networks and alternative sites, It influences people who have historically distrusted doctors.
Activists go door to door to disprove them

"What at first might be perceived as a prioritization of communities of color by doctors actually looks online as, 'Oh, those doctors want us to go first to be guinea pigs,'" said Kolina Koltai. , a University of Washington researcher who studies conspiracy theories online. "I've seen people on Facebook, Twitter, YouTube, the Clubhouse - anywhere - who say the codes are a way to force communities of color to get vaccinated as an experiment."

“The thing about disinformation is that it works best when it is built around something of truth. In this case, many communities of color are wary of the medical system because they haven't had the best experience, ”said Shireen Mitchell, founder of Stop Online Violence Against Women, a group that supports women of color who are bullied online.

An experiment conducted in 1943 on nearly 400 black men in Tuskegee, Alabama is one of the most researched examples of medical abuse in the black community. For four decades, scientists observed the men, who knew they were infected with syphilis, but did not offer them treatments so that they could study the progression of the disease. When the experiment came to light in the 1970s it was condemned by the medical community as a major violation of ethical standards.

Native American groups have been battling vaccination fears in their communities, and doctors say some Chinese-American patients come forward with Chinese-language media articles questioning US-made vaccines.
