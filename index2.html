How will we work when the pandemic passes?

This year he has shown the advantages of remote work. But, if we look at our economic history, it is likely that we will go back to the way we worked before the coronavirus.

In 1957, Isaac Asimov published The Naked Sun , a science fiction novel about a society in which people live on isolated farms, robots meet their needs and only interact through videos. The plot revolves around the way in which this lack of face-to-face contact atrophies and distorts their personalities.

After a year in which those of us were able to work from home - though instead of robots, like the novel, we are served by less fortunate humans - that sounds pretty familiar. But how will we live once the pandemic passes?

No one knows for sure, of course. But perhaps our speculation can be supported by some historical parallels and models. Indeed, a year of isolation gave remote work a classic case of “infant industry” protection, a concept often associated with international trade policy and which was first systematically exposed by none other than Alexander Hamilton.

Hamilton claimed that there were many industries that could flourish in the young America but could not thrive on imports. If these industries were given a breather from competition through temporary tariffs, for example, they could gain enough experience and technological sophistication to become competitive.

The infant industry argument has always been a complicated one as a basis for public policy: how do you know when it is valid? And can we trust governments to make that decision? But the pandemic, by temporarily making our old work habits impossible, has undoubtedly allowed us to take much better advantage of the possibilities of remote work; Plus, some of what we used to do — long trips to sit in cubicles, constant flights to meetings of questionable value — won't come back.

However, if history is any guide, much of our former way of working and living will return.

Here's a parallel: what the internet changed and didn't change in how we read books.

A decade ago, many people believed that both physical books and the bookstores that sold them were on the way to extinction. And part of what they predicted came true: e-readers took a significant share of the market, and large bookstore chains suffered a significant financial hit.

But the popularity of e-books stagnated in the middle of the past decade, never surpassing physical books. And while the big chains have suffered, independent bookstores have flourished.

Why has the reading revolution been so limited? The convenience of downloading e-books is obvious. But for many readers this convenience is offset by more subtle factors. The experience of reading a physical book is different and, for many, more enjoyable than reading in electronic ink. And visiting a bookstore is also a different experience than shopping online. I like to say that on the internet I can find any book I am looking for; in fact, I downloaded a copy of The Naked Sun a few hours before writing this article. However, what I find in a bookstore, especially in a well-preserved independent one, are books that I was not looking for , but that I end up treasuring.

Perhaps the remote work revolution will unfold in a similar way, but on a much larger scale.

The benefits of telecommuting (whether from home or perhaps in small offices located far from dense urban areas) are obvious. Both living and working spaces are getting cheaper; transfers are short or non-existent; You no longer need to deal with the expense and discomfort of formal business wear, at least from the waist down.

Instead, the advantages of returning to face-to-face work will be relatively subtle: the benefits of face-to-face communication, the serendipity that can arise from unscheduled interactions, the comforts of urban life.

Yet these subtle advantages are what drive the economy of modern cities and, until before the advent of COVID-19, these advantages fueled a growing economic divergence between large, highly educated metropolitan areas and the rest of the world. U.S. The rise in telecommuting may curb that trend, but it is unlikely to reverse it.

The resurgence of cities will not be an entirely pleasant process; much of this will perhaps reflect the preferences of wealthy Americans who desire the luxuries and glamor of big cities. "The main problem with moving to Florida is that you have to live in Florida, " a fund manager told Bloomberg. But while cities thrive in part because they adapt to the lifestyles of the rich and fatuous - whether they like it or not, their wealth and power go a long way toward shaping the economy - cities also thrive because much of the trade information and brainstorming takes place during coffee and beer breaks after work hours; Zoom calls are not a suitable substitute.

Or as the great Victorian economist Alfred Marshall said about the technological centers of his time: "The mysteries of the trade cease to be, but they are, so to speak, in the air."

So the best bet is that life and work in, say, 2023 will look a lot like life and work in 2019, but to a lesser extent. We may travel less than we used to to the office; there may be an excess of urban office space. But most of us will not be able to be far from the madding crowd.
